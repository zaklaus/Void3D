Single mesh characters

 

Definitions

 

Single mesh visuals are used mainly for characters, creatures, and other organic bodies, which have ability to smoothly bend parts of their bodies. Insanity has concept of a visual consisting of one continuous mesh, which may be deformed by set of joints.

Technically, single mesh visual is a specialization of I3D_visual object, with special rendering. The visual has attached one or more joints, which are of type I3D_joint.

 

 

 

Joint

 

A single mesh may be deformed by one or more joints. The joints are usually forming a hierarchy. Each joint has defined an affect region, which is an area of vertices in the mesh, which is influenced by the joint. By joint influence we mean how joint’s transformation matrix is applied on selected vertices, or blended with other joint’s transformation matrix on some vertices.

The vertices in the affected area are influenced by the joint by various amount, so that smooth bending is allowed. Additionally, joint influence area expands forward in the mesh, in specified direction. This is used for many parts of body, where joint, located at certain position, affects all vertices from its location until end of body part.

Construction

 

Single mesh characters need properly defined hierarchy of joints affecting the skeleton. This is basic key to proper body deformation, as well as to proper applying of animations on the single mesh body.

The skeleton is made by hierarchically linking joints together, and finally linking them to the single mesh visual.

Note that there’re not joints for head, palms or foots. This is because joints, unlike in some modeling packages, do not represent bones, but really mean joints. Anyway, your characters may have heads and hands, because those are just plain vertices fully influenced by first joint connecting them with the body, for example neck or wrist.

 

Building skeleton

It is important to build proper skeleton for your single mesh character. You should define properly initial positions and rotations of your joints. Mainly rotation is very important – when the skeleton is reset (which means that all joints are set to zero rotation), it should be in a reasonable pose, ideally a base standing pose.

Also names of joints should be carefully chosen, because they’re important for animations, as well as other purposes.

 

Modeling skeleton

The skeleton is made of joints. We’ll assume `that 3D Studio MAX is used for modeling now. The joints are modeled directly in modeling package, using provided plugin supporting Insanity joints. The joints may be easily visualized, using helping geometry, directly in MAX. But note that this geometry is just a visualization hint, so that artist have an idea how each limb is connected to joint. The joint’s visual look may be adjusted to best represent the skeleton. Notice on the image below, that each joint’s visual shape has a fin on one side, so that it’s rotation is easily determined also without real character geometry.

Once you have prepared the skeleton, you may use if for animations, and insert it into character’s geometry.

 

Modeling geometry

Geometry is a visual representation of a character. The geometry of single mesh is best modeled in a base pose, with hands stretched (see pose on Image 3). This is because this pose is better for definition of affect regions in arms, and vertices of hands do not get near vertices of body.

Connecting single mesh geometry with skeleton

 

Once you have completed the skeleton (probably one for entire project), and modeled character’s geometry, it is time to connect those two. After importing both skeleton and geometry into modeling package, you must adjust skeleton to fit into the geometry, so that joints are positioned accurately. Now you must define affect regions, as shown on Image 1. The affect regions are basically bounding boxes, enclosing affected vertices.
Following image illustrates real situation, where joints of elbow and wrist are fit into geometry, and bounding boxes are defined.

Technically, the bounding boxes are done using dummy objects, which are available in most modeling packages. In 3DS Max, dummy object has a bounding box, which may be non-uniformly scaled, so that required shape is achieved. The dummy object will be used only as a helper, and will be removed when loading the single mesh by Insanity. It must have following characteristics: into user-defined properties, type keyword {region}, which means that it defines the morphing region, and you must link the dummy object to the joint, whose affect region it represents.

Additionally, you may specify various shapes of the region by adding integer number after the region keyword. Following types are recognized:

-          0 … standard region, changing influence on vertices along its local Z axis

-          1 … region affecting all vertices inside of it at 100%

-          2 … ellipsoid region, having linear fading influence from its center towards boundary

-          3 … ellipsoid region, similar to 2), but the influence has sine-based fading from center

 

One important thing to note is the way how we rotate the bounding box. Important is dummy’s local axis Z (in 3DS MAX it’s axis Y), which define the direction, how blend ratio is spread, and how vertices are assigned to particular joint. The positive orientation of the axis specifies direction where vertices will be 100% assigned to given joint. Also the scale of bounding box along this axis is important, because when vertex is inside the bounding box, the ratio where it appears along the Z axis specifies the ratio of vertex assignment to given joint’s matrix, or to joint’s parent matrix. In the Image 4, both bounding boxes have the local Z axis oriented to the left.

Locked joints

 

When animating single mesh character, usually it is sufficient to animate rotation, and not position, in order to avoid changing the length of bone connecting two joints (which in reality cannot change).

For this reason, it is possible to explicitly lock some joints, so that their position (set initially during loading) cannot be changed in any way. All attempts to call I3D_frame::SetPos will fails.

To do so, add keyword lock into joint’s properties in modeling package.

 

 

Collisions testing

 

Collision testing on single mesh characters is not as straightforward as for other objects. Testing against real geometry, which is bended by many matrices, would be very non-optimal, anyway it is desired to detect quite accurately collision with characters, mainly if they are shot or hit.

So we use an approximation. Each limb (or bone) is approximated by a capsule – a cylinder with spherical ends. This shape is ideal for characters, which usually have rounded shapes.

Again, we use a helper bounding box, modeled as a dummy object, appropriately oriented and linked to particular joint. The relative offset and rotation to the joint is preserved. The scale of the bounding box specifies the size of the capsule. Dummy’s local Z axis (Y axis in MAX) represents the main capsule’s axis, while other two axes (averaged if they do not match) are used as diameter of the capsule.

The characteristics of the dummy objects are: it must have keyword {JTVB} in the user-defined properties (which stands for Joint volume box), and it must be linked to joint, whose volume (or whose bone’s volume) it represents.

Limits

 

Since entire rendering of single mesh characters is performed by vertex shaders, usually directly on graphics hardware, there’re limits on number of joints which may affect one single mesh. The limit is currently 17 joints per mesh. This amount is sufficient for typical character, but if we decide to model fingers and face using the single mesh technique, the number of joints on single mesh may easily grow to 40 or more. For this reason, it is possible to tell the system, that single mesh consists of more joints than limit allows, and should be rendered differently, The difference in rendering is that single mesh is rendered on multiple passes, each time one segment of maximal amount of 17 joints is rendered.

To achieve this, you must mark parts, where character breaks and which will be rendered as one piece. This marking is very straightforward – simply add break keyword into user-defined properties of particular joint, where you want to break segments of the character. That’s all you shall to do in order to override this (current) hardware limit.

Typically, if you want have a character with fingers and face muscles, you should add the break keyword onto wrist and neck joints. Then, each segment from wrist may have another 17 joints, and face segment starting from neck joint also may have another 17 joints.

 

Anyway, keep in mind that by breaking segments of your character, the performance will be lower then as if entire character was rendered as one segment. So if you plan to render a battle field consisting of tens of character, you should consider not giving them joints onto head or finger. Anyway, if you’re doing an animation with very fine characters, you’ll probably want all fingers be animated, and many muscles being animated on the face, so the use of the possibility is appropriate in this case.

 

 

Animations

 

Single mesh characters are one of most common animated objects in a game. There’re usually many characters in the game (with various single mesh geometry), but many of the character use the same set of animations. Also, the number of animations in games may be high – hundreds or thousands. In order to make a compact and flexible animation files, only the skeleton is animated and kept in the animation files. Refer to the Animations section for description how animations are applied onto models.
